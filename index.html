Laboratory of Computational Biology and Functional Genomics

Our Goals

Our research goal is to understand human genome by employing high throughput sequencing approaches together with computational analysis.
We use genomic/epigenomic datasets to characterize non-coding regulatory sequences. See our three specific research topics below.

Research Topics

The central question in post-GWAS (Genome Wide Association Studies) and personalized medicine era is how we interpret the regulatory effect of genetic variants. Many important genetic variants related to human disease have been discovered, but linking them to disease mechanisms remains a challenge. Since their majority lies within non-coding regions, and their regulatory mechanisms take place via long-range DNA looping interactions, predicting their funcitonal effect is very difficult.

The Effect of Higher-Order Chromatin Structure on Gene Regulation

Understanding higher-order chromatin structure is essential to dissect gene regulatory mechanisms. Despite many recent, we still know little about its effect on gene regulation mechanisms. Many fundamental questions remain to be solved such as whether dynamic higher-order chromatin structure is the cause or effect of gene regulation, whether higher-order chromatin structure directs cellular identity, and so on. We aim to address these unsolved questions by employing 3C based proximity ligation approaches including Hi-C, capture Hi-C, and mathematical modeling of higher-order chromatin structure.

Small Scale / Single Cell Genomics

Current genomic technologies are limited to merely presenting the 'average' properties obtained from cell population. To better understand genomic/epigenomic functions, cell-to-cell variations must be investigated. Development of small scale / single cell (epi)genomic experimental methods and new computational methods to interpret these data become critical to precisely understand gene regulation mechanisms at single cell level.

Neuroepigenomics

Several thousand genetic variants associated with brain disorders have been identified from genome-wide association studies (GWAS). However, it is unknown how epigenetic regulations affect brain disorders. To answer this questions, we hypothesize that gene regulatory networks that play a role in brain disorders can be uncovered by analyzing epigenetic modifications at particular non-coding genomic elements and by predicting the regulatory effect of genetic variants.
